01 The Setup

The web app was the one investors used.

CNBC TV18's web product worked. Sensex on top. Sector heat. IPO calendar. Stock pages two taps deep. The mobile app was a different product altogether — a feed of headlines, with a faint promise of market data buried somewhere in a hamburger menu.

That mismatch wasn't an accident. The web team had spent years building a research surface. The mobile team had built a news reader. Both teams owned different audiences in their heads.

In early 2026, CNBC announced a brand refresh. Digital-first. Markets-first. The mobile app was now expected to carry a positioning the team hadn't designed for. That was the brief.

My role.

I led research and design. The design director provided top-level oversight on direction. Two PMs scoped the build. Three engineers and two QA built and shipped what we cleared. The CPO weighed in at key milestones.

Bottom navigation went into development before I left the company. The rest of the redesign was queued behind it.

What I had to work with.

No primary research. No interviews, no usability tests. The budget and calendar didn't allow for it.

What I did have: app store reviews on the four largest competitor apps, public traffic data, competitor teardowns, and the brand refresh as a forcing function. I worked through that material for several weeks before drawing a line and starting the IA.

What came out of it was a reframe. That's the next section.

As we're not speaking with any users in person, these reviews will help us identify the problems in our application, and in the competitors apps — which later we can avoid repeating on our app designs.

02 Reading The Competition

A research lab built from app stores.

I picked four apps to study: MoneyControl, ET Money, Live Mint, and Zee Business. Plus one secondary: ET Markets. These are the apps a CNBC TV18 user already has on their phone — same audience, different brands.

For each, I did three things. Read the Play Store reviews — hundreds of them — and tagged the themes that came up more than once. Wrote a strengths/weaknesses pass: what the app did better than CNBC, and what it failed at. Then drew a third column — opportunities for us. Not what to copy. What gaps the competitor's failure left for us to take.

Public traffic data sat alongside this. MoneyControl topped Indian financial sites at roughly 10 crore unique visitors per article on its biggest pieces. That number wasn't a target. It was a calibration — this is the audience size at stake, and mobile is where most of them now live.

Two things stood out.

First, the dominant negative wasn't a UI complaint. It was a trust complaint. Problem getting updated data — that's the most damaging thing an investor app can hear. If the price on screen might be five minutes old, you stop relying on the app for decisions. You open another one.

Second, every competitor had some version of this complaint. Which meant the bar to clear wasn't visual polish. It was making the app feel now. Real-time as the default state, not a flag the user had to verify. That insight changed the brief.

The honest constraint.

I didn't talk to a single user during this work. I read what they wrote on app store pages — which is a particular kind of evidence. It's biased toward extremes, biased toward people who write reviews, and captures a moment rather than a relationship.

But the patterns repeated. Across four apps, dozens of reviews, weeks of reading. When the same complaint shows up across competitors that don't share product teams, that's not bias. That's a category problem. And category problems are the ones a redesign can actually solve.

03 The Reframe

News readers scan for novelty. Investors scan for deviation.

— I.
Mental model — News reader

Scans for novelty.

"What's new since the last time I checked." Top story. Latest news. More stories below. Personalisation as an afterthought.

— II.
Mental model — Investor

Scans for deviation.

"What's moved away from where I expected it to be." The same content can serve both — but only if the layout serves both.

The existing CNBC mobile app had been built around scanning for novelty. That's a fine layout for a news app. It's the wrong layout for an investor.

The old IA put News on the home tab. Markets was a section you navigated to. The redesign inverts that: Markets becomes the surface the app opens on. News becomes the section you navigate to.

This sounds like a small move. It isn't. It changes who the app is for by default. A news-first app politely makes room for investor tools. A markets-first app politely makes room for news. The polite-room-making is what determines how the app feels in the first three seconds — and the first three seconds are what the user remembers.

"Events" as a top-level tab. CNBC's premium events business — summits, awards, industry conferences — was the brand's most underused commercial asset on mobile. It deserved a tab, not a section inside News. Made discoverable, ticket attach could become a real lever rather than a rounding error.

"For you" instead of "Portfolio." Most apps in this category use "Portfolio" or "My Stocks". The problem with "Portfolio" is that it implies the user has done the setup work. For the aspiring-investor segment we wanted to court, "Portfolio" reads as for someone else. "For you" grows with the user instead of asking them to be ahead of where they are.

Three personas. One cut.

The redesign was scoped for three. We had drafted a fourth — the dedicated trader — and removed them.

PERSONA 01

Active retail investor

Already trades. Wants fast, accurate, current. Comes for the data first, the analysis second.

PERSONA 02

Aspiring investor & learner

New or casual. Wants the path to feel obvious and the data to feel safe. Will leave if intimidated.

PERSONA 03

Business enthusiast

Doesn't trade much. Wants to feel informed. Reads more, taps less.

The dedicated trader's tooling needs (advanced charts, options chains, technical indicators) would have demanded a different product. Trying to serve them inside this app would have either bloated the experience for the other three, or under-served the trader badly. You either pick who you're for, or you let everyone in and please nobody.

04 Four Pillars

Each principle had a decision and an alternative we killed.

The trend analysis came out as four design principles. Each was anchored in something the research had surfaced. Each had a decision attached, and each had something we considered and didn't do.

— I.

Navigation.

Discoverability · Hierarchy

Buried features were the consistent theme across reviews of the old CNBC app. IPO data, events, the live TV stream — all sat behind hamburger menus or deep section pages. MoneyControl's same complaint was its biggest weakness in the strengths/weaknesses pass.

Decision

Bottom navigation with five tabs. L1 sub-navigation within each tab. The hamburger reduced to settings, account, and help — secondary surfaces only.

Considered and killed

A four-tab bar with a docked "+" in the middle for primary actions. Killed because the audience was already complaining about hidden features. A modal action sheet behind a generic "+" would be one more hidden mode, not one fewer.

— II.

Readability.

Typography · Numeric scanning

The old app used Montserrat across the board. Geometric, modern, brand-consistent. And wrong for the product. Montserrat's narrow numerals and tight numeric line-height make stock prices, percentages, and indices harder to scan than they need to be at the small sizes the app uses.

Decision

Inter for UI and numeric data — built for screens, with a tall x-height that holds up at 12–14pt. Noto Serif for long-form articles, where the eye benefits from a proper reading face.

Considered and killed

Keeping Montserrat for headlines and using Inter only for data. Killed because the brand should serve the product, not the other way around. Mixed-typeface stacks read inconsistent, and the app's primary content is numbers more often than headlines.

— III.

Personalisation.

Default state · Memory

The reviews of competitor apps that worked shared one feature: the user's content was waiting for them when they opened the app. ET Money's watchlist. Live Mint's saved sectors. The CNBC app had none of this.

Decision

Watchlist as the default state on the "For you" tab. Stock alerts. Sector preferences inferred from articles read. The app remembers between sessions — the user doesn't reconfigure on every launch.

Considered and killed

A persistent watchlist strip across the top of the home (Markets) tab. Killed because the home tab needs to communicate the state of the market first. The user's personal positions are second-order. That's what "For you" is for.

— IV.

Accessibility.

Dark mode · Font scaling

CNBC TV18 had a font-scaling toggle in settings. It was technically present, functionally broken — most screens didn't respect it. The app shipped without dark mode at all, which is a meaningful gap for an audience that opens the app early-morning and late-evening, often in low light.

Decision

Dark mode as a real surface — not a tinted overlay, a properly tokenised parallel theme. Font scaling tied to system settings, respected on every screen.

Considered and killed

Building our own font-scale slider inside the app, separate from the OS-level setting. Killed because users who need accessibility have already configured it at the system level. Asking them to set it again, inside one app, fragments the experience.

A claim about the IA, not about user behaviour. The reduction is verifiable from the design itself — counting screens between app open and a stock detail page, before and after.

What I'd measure.

If users behave on the new IA the way they do on comparable apps after similar reductions, the engagement lift on those flows lands in the 30–40% range. Whether they do is the open question. Post-launch metrics weren't accessible to me after I left, so I'm not making a behavioural claim here. A measurement plan, in place of metrics I can't claim:

Leading indicators — within a week
Time-to-first-quote

Seconds from app open to a stock detail page. Direct test of the "Markets first" thesis.

Watchlist creation rate

Share of new sessions that add at least one stock in the first three uses. Proxy for the personalisation thesis.

Events tab tap-through

Share of sessions that touch the Events tab. Tests whether premium events have become discoverable.

Lagging indicators — over a quarter
D7 / D30 retention by persona segment

Most important and most expensive to instrument. Tells us if the right people are coming back.

Event ticket attach rate

The commercial test. Did surfacing Events as a tab move tickets?

Session count — market hours vs. after-hours

Tells us whether the app has become an investor surface, or stayed a news reader.

06 What I'd Do Differently

The questions I never got to answer.

— I.

I'd run user interviews — not as a luxury, as a structural check.

The reframe (Markets first instead of News first) is sound for active investors. I never got to test whether it landed for the aspiring-investor segment. That segment might feel intimidated by a Markets-first home.

Eight to ten thirty-minute conversations would have answered that. I didn't have the budget at the time. The case for the budget would be sharper now: a redesign that got the active investor right and lost the aspirer would have been a strategic miss, not a design success.

— II.

I'd think harder about the trader segment before cutting them.

Cutting the dedicated trader was right for this version. But ceding the highest-LTV user to broker apps is also a long-term commercial concession.

There's a product question CNBC TV18 will eventually face — does it grow into a tradeable product, or stay forever on the consumption side? That decision was above my pay grade. It's still the one that determines whether "For you" grows up into something that monetises seriously, or remains an editorial surface.

— III.

I'd seriously question whether Markets and News should even be separate tabs.

If I were rebuilding this from scratch with no legacy taxonomy, I'd consider collapsing them. An investor experiencing a market move during the move doesn't separate "the price changed" from "why it changed."

The redesign honoured the existing news/markets split because that's how the editorial team is organised. A bolder version would treat them as one surface and let context do the rest. That's the version I didn't get to make.

— Closing

A real attempt at a real strategic mismatch.

The CNBC TV18 mobile redesign used the evidence available, drew lines where lines were honest, and shipped what could be shipped on the timeline that existed.

The bottom navigation that did ship is the part I'm most willing to let stand alone — it changed the structural answer to a question that was costing the brand investor attention every day the old app was open. The rest is a plan, not an outcome.

That's the version of the work that's true.
